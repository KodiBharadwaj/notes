 Computer networks basics 
 
1) What is DNS(Domain Name System)
	
Certainly! Let's break down DNS in simpler terms:

Naming Websites: 
Think of the internet like a huge city with many houses. Each house needs a unique address for people to find it. 
Similarly, websites on the internet have unique addresses called IP addresses (like phone numbers for computers).
	
Domain Names:
Instead of remembering complex IP addresses, we use domain names (e.g., www.example.com). It's like using names instead 
of phone numbers to contact friends.
	
DNS is Like a Phonebook:
DNS is like a giant phonebook for the internet. When you type a domain name in your browser, DNS helps find the correct
IP address associated with that name.
	
How It Works:
Imagine you want to visit a website (e.g., www.example.com). Your computer asks a DNS system, "Hey, what's the IP address for www.example.com?"
The DNS system checks its records and says, "It's this IP address: 192.168.1.1."
Now, your computer knows where to find the website, just like knowing a friend's address.

DNS Servers:
DNS servers are like friendly librarians who help you find information. Some are big librarians (like Google's DNS), 
and some are local librarians (your ISP's DNS). 
	
Caching:
To make things faster, your computer remembers (caches) the IP addresses it gets from DNS. If you visit the same website again, 
your computer already knows the address.
	
Hierarchy: 

DNS is organized like a big tree. At the top are the root servers, then domain registrars (like city planners), 
and finally, individual websites (like houses).
In summary, DNS is like an internet address book. It helps your computer find the right address (IP) for websites you want to visit, 
making the internet more user-friendly.


 
 
 


2) What is a firewall?
Imagine a Security Guard for Your Computer:

A firewall is like a security guard for your computer or network. It watches over the traffic coming in and going out, 
deciding what is safe and what is not. 

Building a Barrier:
Think of your computer or network as a castle. The firewall builds a protective barrier around it to keep bad things out.
	
Checking Every Visitor:
When data (like messages or files) wants to enter or leave your castle (computer), the firewall checks it. 
It asks, "Are you safe? Do I know you?" If the answer is yes, it allows the data through. If not, it blocks it.
		
Guarding Against Bad Stuff:
Sometimes, there are bad things on the internet (like viruses or hackers). The firewall is like a superhero guard, 
protecting your computer from these bad things.
	
Keeping Records:
The firewall keeps a record of everything that happens. It's like a security log that helps figure out what went wrong if there's ever a problem.
In simple terms, a firewall is like a digital security guard that watches over your computer, checks who is coming and going, and ensures that only 
the good stuff gets in while keeping the bad stuff out. It's an essential part of keeping your computer safe on the internet.
